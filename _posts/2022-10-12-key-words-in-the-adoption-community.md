<h4>Slide text</h4>

<ul>
  <li>
    <strong>Slide one</strong><br>
    <strong>Key words in the adoption community</strong><br>
    Transracial adoptee, international adoptee, and more
  </li>

  <li>
    <strong>Slide two</strong><br>
    <strong>Adoptee</strong><br>
    The person who was adopted. Some people use “adoptee” as a way of identifying.
  </li>

  <li>
    <strong>Slide three</strong><br>
    <strong>Adopted</strong><br>
    This can be used to describe the person who was adopted. It emphasizes the action of adoption. Some people also use it as an identifier.
  </li>

  <li>
    <strong>Slide four</strong><br>
    <strong>Transracial adoptee</strong><br>
    A person who was adopted into a family that identifies as a different race than themselves.
  </li>

  <li>
    <strong>Slide five</strong><br>
    <strong>International adoptee</strong><br>
    A person who was adopted into a country different from the one where they were born. Someone can be a transracial adoptee, an international adoptee, both, or neither.
  </li>

  <li>
    <strong>Slide six</strong><br>
    <strong>Transcontinental adoptee</strong><br>
    A person who was adopted into a different continent than the one where they were born.
  </li>

  <li>
    <strong>Slide seven</strong><br>
    <strong>Person-first vs. identity-first language</strong><br>
    <em>Person-first:</em> Téa, who is a transracial adoptee, was born in China.<br>
    <em>Identity-first:</em> Transracial adoptee Téa was born in China.
  </li>
</ul>
